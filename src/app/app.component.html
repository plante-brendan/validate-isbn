International Standard Book Numbers (ISBN) come in two forms. Ten digit ISBN were used from their creation in 1970 until 2007. A ten digit ISBN has 4 sections. Sections can all be crammed together with no divider, or you can use space or dash. Section 1 can be up to 5 characters, section 2 can be up to 7 characters, section 3 can be up to 6 characters, and section 4 is the last digit only. Each digit may only be 0-9 except the last one which can also be “X”. Additionally, you may add ISBN or ISBN-10 with or without a colon to the start of the ISBN as long as a space is added in between this prefix and the first digit of the ISBN.

Thirteen digit ISBN were introduced in 2007 and have been in use ever since. A thirteen digit ISBN has 5 sections. Sections can all be crammed together with no divider, or you can use space or dash. Section 1 must be either 978 or 979, Section 2 can be up to 5 characters, section 3 can be up to 7 characters, section 4 can be up to 6 characters, and section 5 is the last digit only. Each digit of the ISBN must be 0-9, there is no “X” in 13 digit ISBN. Additionally, you may add ISBN or ISBN-13 with or without a colon to the start of the ISBN as long as a space is added in between this prefix and the first digit of the ISBN. Sections can all be crammed together with no divider, or you can use space or dash.
